Study Habits

It is not about how long you study — it is about how well.

Studying is not always difficult because the work is hard, but because many students struggle with how to study effectively. Common problems include forgetting information quickly, feeling overwhelmed by large amounts of content, and spending long hours studying without seeing results. This page explores simple study habits and techniques that can help.

What Effective Studying Means

Effective studying is not about studying for long hours or trying to memorise everything at once. Instead, it focuses on understanding information, reviewing it over time, and building habits that make studying more manageable and less stressful. Good study habits help reduce last-minute cramming and make learning more consistent.

Active Recall

Active recall is a study technique that involves testing yourself on information instead of only re-reading notes or textbooks. This could include answering questions from memory, using flashcards, or explaining a topic out loud without looking at your notes. Although active recall can feel more difficult than passive studying, it helps strengthen memory and improves long-term understanding.

Examples of active recall include:

  • Answering practice questions from memory
  • Covering notes and trying to recall key points
  • Explaining a topic as if you were teaching someone else

Spaced Repetition

Spaced repetition involves reviewing information over increasing periods of time instead of studying everything at once. Rather than cramming before a test, material is revisited several times across days or weeks. This method helps improve long-term memory and reduces the chances of forgetting information quickly.

Spaced repetition can be done by:

  • Reviewing notes a few days after first learning them
  • Using flashcards regularly
  • Scheduling short revision sessions over time

Short and Focused Study Sessions

Long study sessions can lead to tiredness and loss of focus. Studying in short, focused sessions is often more effective. One common approach is studying for about 25 minutes, followed by a short break. Short sessions help maintain concentration and make studying feel more manageable, especially for students who struggle with motivation.

Building Consistent Study Habits

Studying becomes easier when it is done consistently. Studying at the same time each day, even for a short period, helps turn studying into a habit rather than something that requires motivation. Small, regular study sessions are often more effective than occasional long sessions.

There is no single study method that works perfectly for everyone. The techniques on this page are starting points that students can experiment with and adjust to suit their own learning styles.
